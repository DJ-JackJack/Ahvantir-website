# Solara and Nystara

Solara and Nystara are Nyhexus's twin suns — a binary star system around which the planet orbits on a circumbinary path, encircling both stars at once. Solara is the larger of the two: a warm yellow star whose light most inhabitants of Aru'Mas simply call *daylight*. Nystara is smaller, burns blue-white, and runs far hotter than her counterpart. Both have shaped Ahvantir's calendar, its agriculture, its military iconography, and the symbolic vocabulary the city reaches for when it needs to talk about two things that are not the same.

---

## The Stars

### Solara

Solara is the larger sun and the more dominant one in practical terms. Her light is warm and yellow — the color the eye defaults to when it pictures a sun. Her disc in the sky is visibly larger than Nystara's. Her output drives the agricultural seasons, determines planting and harvest windows, and sets the effective temperature of Ahvantir's climate across the year.

The name is older than any surviving written record — it predates the [[Marducian Calendar]] and the [[First Pact]], and almost certainly predates the settler fleet's arrival entirely. Whether the [[Drorn'Duur]] or the Ahvantiri spirits used the same name is unrecorded. The settlers adopted it, or found it already in use, and it has not changed since.

Solara's name is everywhere in Aru'Mas. The [[Sunspear Legion]]'s signature weapons — enchanted spears issued to Ironshield soldiers — are warded to emit a light that Legion records describe as *matching Solara's character*: steady, warm, and capable of holding a line. The [[Citadel of Solara]], the Legion's headquarters in Stonegate, takes her name. The first day of the Marducian week, Solkir, is named after her and associated with vitality and new beginnings. The **Dawn of Solara** is the Legion's primary civic festival. When Aru'Mas wants to mark peacekeeping and civic order, it reaches for Solara.

### Nystara

Nystara is smaller in apparent disc size and burns hotter. She is blue-white — visibly cooler in color tone than Solara when both are seen at once, which is counterintuitive to observers who assume the bluer star must be the colder one. The opposite is true. Nystara's surface temperature runs well above Solara's, and her output skews heavily toward blue and ultraviolet. Looking directly at Nystara is more dangerous than looking at Solara, which most people discover by making the mistake once.

She is not, by most measures, the lesser of the pair. Solara dominates the visual impression of Ahvantir's sky by disc size alone; a smaller disc does not mean a smaller contribution. What Nystara lacks in breadth she makes up in intensity.

The [[Vanguard of Valor]] — the [[Sunspear Legion]]'s heavy-assault division, called upon when peacekeeping has already failed — bear Nystara's emblem on their armor. The symbolism is explicit in Legion records: Ironshields carry weapons that glow with Solara's light; the Vanguard carry a mark that does not glow and is not meant to comfort. Nystara's association in Aru'Mas is with resolve, endurance under pressure, and the kind of refusal to yield that does not make for comfortable festivals. She is invoked quietly. She does not parade.

## Orbital Mechanics

Ahvantir orbits both stars together on a path around their shared gravitational center — a circumbinary orbit, in the vocabulary of Arrandak Academy astronomers. The two suns are never dramatically separated in the sky. They move as a pair, one leading or trailing the other by a margin that shifts over time, but always rising and setting within the same general arc.

Solara and Nystara orbit each other on a cycle of roughly eight Marducian years — long enough that the change is invisible month to month, visible across a year, and unmistakable after three or four. At the cycle's widest point there is a clear gap between them: farmers use it as a seasonal gauge, the way they read Toris's height above the horizon. As the cycle draws toward its close — the period astronomers call **the Pairing** and most people call *when the suns are together* — the two discs appear nearly adjacent, separated by less than a sun-width. Neither star passes in front of the other from Ahvantir's perspective. It is a visual convergence, not an eclipse: two objects sharing a narrow patch of sky, their halos overlapping, the combined light between them running noticeably brighter than midday average.

The Pairing happens roughly every eight years. It is not catastrophic. It is not magical, officially. It is the subject of a very large body of astrological writing, most of it contradictory.

## Appearance from the Surface

When both suns are in the sky — which covers most daylight hours on most days of the year — their light combines into something cleaner and slightly cooler than either alone would produce. Solara's gold and Nystara's blue-white blend and push the effective color of daylight toward white. Ahvantir's sky does not look like a single-sun sky. It is a shade more vivid, a shade less warm, and the shadows betray it.

Every object in open sunlight casts two shadows. One is warmer and softer, angled from Solara's position in the sky. The other is sharper and edged toward pale blue, angled from Nystara's. Where both shadows overlap, a deeper shadow forms — darker than either alone and noticeably cooler in color. Where neither shadow falls, the combined light is at its brightest. The effect is most visible on textured surfaces — bark, dressed stone, woven cloth — where the two-shadow geometry produces a depth that takes new eyes a moment to parse.

Inhabitants of Aru'Mas do not consciously register this. They grew up with it. Travelers from single-sun worlds often find the twin-shadow effect the first alien thing they notice about Ahvantir, more immediate than the moons, the magic, or the walls. There is a persistent joke among experienced [[Starleaper Fleet|Starleaper]] guides that you can spot a newcomer on a clear day by watching where their eyes go.

At dawn and dusk, the character of the light shifts based on which sun reaches the horizon first — and which one does varies by season and orbital phase. If Solara sets first, Nystara closes the day in pale blue and sharp edges. If Nystara sets first, Solara brings the evening down in amber and rust. Over the course of a year, both endings happen.

## The Pale Hour

When Nystara is in the sky and Solara is not — either at dawn before Solara has risen or in the evening after she has set — the period of single-blue-sun light is called the Pale Hour. The name is traditional rather than exact; the span runs anywhere from twenty minutes to nearly two hours depending on season and the current orbital phase.

Pale Hour light is cooler and harder than combined daylight. Color perception shifts: warm tones flatten, cool tones sharpen. Shadows become crisper. Distance reads more cleanly. There is a clinical quality to it that the inhabitants of Aru'Mas associate, at a level that has become cultural reflex, with precision and uncomfortable clarity.

This association is old enough to have filtered into professional practice. Certain apothecaries and surgeons of older Aru'Mas scheduled precision work during dawn Pale Hours. Military dispatches from the [[History of Ahvantir Landing War (-13 to 0 MC)|Landing War]] record officers deliberately waiting for Pale Hour conditions to review plans approved in warmer light the night before. Whether the light actually improves judgment or whether this is four centuries of pattern-making layered onto a visual quirk is a question neither scholars nor practitioners have ever fully resolved. The practice persists regardless.

The equivalent period for Solara alone — Nystara below the horizon, Solara still up — has no dedicated name. It is simply the warm light, unremarkable and familiar, the color most people picture when they close their eyes and imagine the end of the day.
