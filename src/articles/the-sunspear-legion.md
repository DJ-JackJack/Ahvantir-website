## Overview

The Sunspear Legion is the standing military of [[Aru'Mas]] — its city guard, its ceremonial standard-bearer, and the institution that more than any other has carried the founding generation's name into the present day. Four hundred and thirty-nine years after the signing of the [[First Pact]], soldiers still drill in the Citadel of Solara bearing weapons named after the founder, swearing oaths by his memory, and patrolling a city built on history they are not taught in full.

The Legion is widely respected. It is also, in ways few people have the context to recognize, a monument to selective memory.

It is the oldest continuous institution in the city. It predates the [[Ilderas Dynasty]] by an unknown span of generations. Those structures are inherited artifacts from the Landing War. They have been maintained. They have not been questioned.

## Founding

The Sunspear Legion was not created by decree. It grew — and then, at a specific moment, it was named.

When [[Marduk Sunspear]] arrived on Ahvantir's shores with the remnants of a refugee fleet — roughly 680 able-bodied adults by the military tally taken at that shore — he did not have an army. He had a ship's crew, a collection of survivors from other worlds, and the desperate discipline of people who had spent forty-nine years adrift in the Astral Sea. What organization existed was the organization of necessity: those who could fight did, those who knew tactics led, and the rest followed.

### The Spear

In the earliest years of the [[History of Ahvantir Landing War (-13 to 0 MC)|Landing War]], Marduk's closest fighting retinue had no formal name. They were simply *the people who guarded him* — the inner ring of warriors who had been with him longest, trusted most, and were never far from his side. Somewhere in those early years of conflict, before the settler coalition had cohered into anything resembling a military institution, this retinue acquired a name: **The Spear**.

The name was a direct reference to Marduk's artifact weapon — the spear he carried throughout the war, already legendary among the settlers who watched him fight. To be part of The Spear was to be part of his instrument. It was a personal guard in the truest sense: not an institution, not a command hierarchy, but an extension of the man himself.

The Spear held together across thirteen years of the Landing War — through the [[Massacre of Cascading Leaves]], through the [[Battle of Hallowed Hollow]], through the long middle years when the outcome was genuinely uncertain. Their cohesion was not organizational but personal. Marduk held them together. His will was their structure.

### From Regiment to Institution

The [[First Pact]] changed everything. When the treaty was signed at Year 0 MC, ending the Landing War and establishing the legal basis for Aru'Mas, the city it created required more than a personal retinue. The Pact mandated control of the [[Spiritsway Passage]] — the only known safe crossing through the [[Mountain Wall]] — and the city of Aru'Mas grew around the obligation to defend and regulate it. Maintaining a standing military capable of that defense was a treaty requirement, not a political choice.

The Spear could not fulfill that role as it was. A personal guard is loyal to a person; an institution must outlast one. In the months and years following the Pact's signing, The Spear was formalized, expanded, and restructured into something that could function without Marduk at its center. It absorbed other fighting units from the settler coalition. It developed a command hierarchy that reported to the new city's governing structure rather than directly to one man. It became, in the language of the treaty obligations it was built to fulfill, the **Sunspear Legion**.

The name honored Marduk, who was still alive at the time of the Legion's formal establishment — the last great act of naming before his death in the post-founding period, the details of which have remained vague in official records ever since. Whether Marduk chose the name himself, or whether his companions chose it for him, is not recorded. What is recorded is that the members of The Spear who formed the Legion's original core kept that prior identity close. The personal oath they had taken to their captain became the institutional oath the Legion still administers — the words revised, but the structure of loyalty unchanged.

## The Name and the Spear

There are two objects in Aru'Mas called a "sunspear."

The first is a relic: Marduk Sunspear's actual spear, held in the Citadel of Solara and displayed in the Magister of Defense's quarters as the Legion's founding symbol. On the [[Day of Unity]], it is carried through the streets in ceremonial procession by the sitting Magister of Defense — a tradition that dates to the early post-founding period and has never been formally interrupted. The relic is the Legion's aquila: its sacred object, its identity made physical.

The second is a weapon: an enchanted spear carried by Ironshield soldiers, warded to glow with the light of Solara and capable of dispelling dark energies. These weapons are produced by Legion armorers, blessed by chaplains of the [[Order of Valor]], and issued to qualifying soldiers as part of their formal kit. Every Ironshield who completes training carries one.

The convergence is not accidental. The Legion's institutional identity was deliberately constructed around Marduk's name and image in the same years when the official founding narrative was being assembled from politically useful pieces of the truth. The Legion became one of the mechanisms through which that narrative was sustained: soldiers bearing Marduk's name, performing his memory in ceremony, trained in his values. They do not typically interrogate his history.

This is not a conspiracy. There is no one person orchestrating it. It is what institutions do when they are built on myths — they perpetuate them through ritual and repetition, through a rank that carries a founder's name, through four centuries of *this is simply how it is done.*

## Command Structure

### High Command

The Legion is led by the **Magister of Defense**, who reports directly to the ruling council of Aru'Mas. The appointment is made by the monarch but requires confirmation from both the [[Merchants Guild|Merchants' Guild]] and [[The Church of the Threefold Path|the Church of the Threefold Path]] — a tripartite structure that ensures no single faction controls the military outright.

This appointment mechanism was not always formal. In the Sunspear succession era, the Magister of Defense's role was filled by whoever Marduk's line trusted — a matter of personal loyalty to the dynasty, not law.

That changed violently. During **Asgoraviel's siege** of the city (~330–339 MC, nine years), the last king of the Sunspear line, **Dammadan Sunspear**, was killed. His entire court fell with him. The sole survivor was his queen consort, **Nishta Ilderas-Sunspear**, who claimed the throne in the aftermath and held the city together through the siege's conclusion. The [[Ilderas Dynasty]] dates its official founding not from Nishta's emergency rule but from the subsequent coronation of **Galligan Ilderas** — her son, and the first to bear the Ilderas name alone.

It was during this transition that the appointment mechanism was codified. Nishta had every reason to want the Magister of Defense's office formalized — a dynasty whose claim to power rested on military survival and an interrupted line of succession needed the military to answer to institutions, not to persons. The tripartite confirmation structure that requires both the Merchants' Guild and the Church to ratify the appointment is her legacy as much as Galligan's. The current system has held for nearly a century.

> **See Also**
> The full history of this transition — Asgoraviel's nine-year siege (~330–339 MC), Dammadan's death, Nishta's interim claim, and Galligan's coronation — is developed in [[The Fall of the Sunspear Line]].
Supporting the Magister of Defense is the **Council of Spears**: senior officers representing each of the Legion's five divisions. The Council meets regularly to assess threats, coordinate defense, and advise on promotions within their units. In practice, the Council also serves as a check on the Magister of Defense — no major operational deployment is undertaken without Council awareness, and a unanimous Council vote can refer a Magister of Defense to the ruling council for review, though this power has been exercised only twice in recorded history.

Beneath the Magister of Defense and Council are **Garrison Commanders**: district-level officers responsible for maintaining order and managing Legion presence in their assigned area of Aru'Mas. Each major district has at least one; busier districts like [[Stonegate]] and [[Luminous Reach]] run full garrison staffs.

### Ranks Within Divisions

Within each division, rank progresses through four recognized tiers:

**Spearlord** — Division commander or senior unit officer. Equivalent to a captain. Spearlords sit on the Council of Spears as divisional representatives.

**Ironwarden** — Veteran soldier with demonstrated command capacity. Responsible for training junior members and managing small-unit operations. The backbone of institutional knowledge in any division.

**Sunspear** — Full member of the Legion. This is the rank most citizens picture when they imagine a Legion soldier. The title is the Legion's primary identifier; soldiers at this rank are addressed as Sunspear regardless of which division they serve in.

**Initiate** — A recruit in the active training period, enrolled in the Spear Academy. Initiates are not yet full members and do not carry the sunspear weapon until training is complete.

The use of "Sunspear" as both an institutional name and a military rank is deliberate. Every fully trained soldier in the Legion bears Marduk's name as a personal title. Whether that is tribute or burden depends on who you ask — and, more to the point, what they know.

## The Five Divisions

### The Skywardens

The Skywardens are the most visible and celebrated division in the Legion — the aerial cavalry of Aru'Mas. Mounted on flying creatures drawn from four species ([[Stormtalons]], [[Aetherwings]], [[Drakehawks]], and [[Skyblades]]), they patrol the city's airspace, conduct long-range reconnaissance, and serve as the rapid-response force for high-altitude threats and magical incursions.

The division's training is among the most demanding in the Legion: aerial combat, joint tactics with ground forces, arcane communication with mounts, and the complex work of maintaining a bond with a creature that can kill you. Candidates must pass the **Sky Trials** — tests that include fighting from moving mounts, engaging targets mid-air, and surviving high-altitude conditions under stress. Fewer than one in three who begin the Trials finish them. Skywardens who wash out are returned to the general pool and often serve respectably in the Ironshields.

The Skywardens also perform aerial displays during major festivals — not frivolous ceremony, but public demonstrations of what the city can field. Tens of thousands watch from the rooftops. [[House Der'Tagan]]'s Captain Yelra Der'Tagan currently leads the Skywarden division in [[Luminous Reach]], one of the more senior Skywarden postings in Aru'Mas.

### The Ironshields

The Ironshields are the Legion's core infantry: the soldiers that patrol streets, manage gates, staff checkpoints, and respond to civil disturbances. They are the Legion most citizens interact with daily, which makes them simultaneously the institution's public face and its most politically complicated element.

The Ironshields carry warded shields designed to resist both physical and minor magical attacks, and the signature sunspear weapons — enchanted to glow with Solara's light and capable of dispelling certain dark energies. Their training emphasizes anti-magic tactics alongside conventional combat, reflecting the reality that Aru'Mas is a multiversal hub where magical disturbances are routine. A competent Ironshield can hold a district checkpoint for twelve hours and handle everything from a drunken adventurer to a summoned entity, often without escalation.

The Ironshields are the enforcement arm of a city with enormous wealth inequality, and they know which side of that they work on. In [[The Spillway|the Spillway]], the sight of an Ironshield uniform tends to inspire wariness rather than comfort — a fact long-serving soldiers understand and rarely discuss.

### The Nightcloaks

The Nightcloaks are the Legion's intelligence and covert operations division, operating below the public radar in ways most citizens don't know about and Legion officials rarely confirm. Their formal mandate covers espionage, counter-espionage, and the neutralization of internal threats: assassins, rogue mages, organized criminal leadership, and entities like the [[Red Masquerade]] — a vampiric network with significant influence in the city's underworld.

Nightcloaks are equipped with shadow-infused armor capable of blending with darkness, enchanted daggers, and a range of tools for silent operations. Their training in shadow magic and illusion is supplemented by intensive intelligence tradecraft — how to build and maintain covers, how to identify an informant, how to leave a scene without leaving traces that hold up to scrutiny.

The Nightcloaks' relationship to the rest of the Legion is deliberately ambiguous. They do not share operational details with the Council of Spears except in general terms. Their Spearlord reports directly to the Magister of Defense — a chain that keeps the division's work politically legible while maintaining the operational distance its work requires.

### The Runeguard

The Runeguard are the Legion's arcane division: battlemages, warded warriors, and counter-spell specialists deployed against magical threats too complex or dangerous for conventional forces. They handle rogue spellcasters, unstable portals, summoned entities, and the ongoing challenge of operating in a city where the boundary between planes is not always firm.

Working in close coordination with the [[Order of Wisdom]], the Runeguard maintain the largest systematic catalogue of magical threat signatures in the city's history. This catalogue includes entries the Runeguard themselves cannot fully explain — phenomena from the [[Fend and the Fendfolk|Fend]] whose underlying logic traces to agreements the Runeguard have found but cannot source. Some of these phenomena appear to follow patterns of obligation and reciprocity that don't match any known spirit pact from the post-founding period. Members who press this question find their inquiries quietly discouraged.

Their standard equipment includes runic staves, warded armor enchanted to resist magical attack, and spellbreakers — small talismans capable of disrupting hostile magic in close proximity. Several Runeguard Spearlords have published papers through the [[Arrandak Academy]] under pseudonyms.

### The Vanguard of Valor

The Vanguard of Valor are the Legion's shock troops — the heaviest, most offensively oriented division, deployed when decisive force is needed and when subtlety has already failed. They lead charges in major battles, hold the line during sieges, and are the first division called when an external threat reaches the outer walls.

Vanguard armor bears the emblem of Nystara — the second of Ahvantir's twin suns, associated with resolve and the refusal to yield. Where the Ironshields carry weapons that glow with Solara's light, the Vanguard bear Nystara's mark: a deliberate duality running across the Legion's two most visible divisions, the light of one sun for peacekeeping, the emblem of the other for war. Their training is brutal by design — only soldiers already proven in another division are eligible for consideration, and they must pass the **Trials of Radiance**, a series of combat and endurance evaluations designed to simulate extreme crisis scenarios. Vanguards carry great weapons: halberds, mauls, two-handed swords. They are not built for finesse.

The Vanguard carry the Legion's darkest institutional memory in their own particular way. They are the division most directly descended from the fighting structure of the Landing War — the shock force that held territory and broke enemy lines during thirteen years of a war that began with a crime. Some of what was done in those years has no monument.

## Role and Duties

The Legion's operational mandate covers three areas:

**Defense of the city** is the primary duty: street patrols, law enforcement, wall-guarding, gate management, and maintaining order at the [[Spiritsway Passage]]. Garrison Commanders handle day-to-day operations; the Council of Spears escalates anything requiring cross-district coordination. The Spiritsway Passage is the Legion's most strategically critical post — the Pact obligation that justified the Legion's existence from the first day.

**Ceremonial functions** are more significant than they appear. The Legion's presence at major festivals, its aerial displays, and its role guarding official events are not simply pomp — they are the institution's most direct communication with the city's population. The Day of Unity procession of Marduk's spear is the single largest annual gathering in Aru'Mas. The understanding that tens of thousands of people form of what the Legion is, what Marduk represented, and what the city's founding means flows substantially through that single ceremony.

**Specialized and multiversal operations** reflect Aru'Mas's position as a hub. The Legion has operational jurisdiction beyond the city walls: it intervenes in the Fend during significant spiritual disturbances, conducts patrols into the wider archipelago, and undertakes missions requiring planar travel. These are among the most dangerous Legion postings, and they are the postings where the Runeguard most frequently encounters things it cannot explain.

## Training and Recruitment

All recruits enter through the **Spear Academy**, located within the Citadel of Solara in Stonegate. The Academy's curriculum covers martial combat, magical defense, and institutional history. The institutional history curriculum presents the official account.

Recruitment draws from three primary sources: adventurers' guilds, the [[Order of Valor]], and the general population. Adventurers who have demonstrated combat effectiveness are frequently approached; the Order of Valor maintains a formal referral relationship with Legion intake officers. General population recruits are more common than the Legion's elite reputation implies — the Ironshields in particular absorb significant numbers of working-class citizens who see military service as a path to stability.

Selection for elite divisions happens from within the active soldier pool, typically after one to three years of service. The Runeguard recruits specifically from among soldiers who demonstrate magical aptitude during Academy training, with some direct recruitment from [[Arrandak Academy]] graduates.

[[House Der'Tagan]] maintains an especially robust pipeline into the Legion. Der'Tagan children are raised in physical and tactical discipline from an early age; many enter the Spear Academy with preparation equivalent to three years of normal training. The Der'Tagan compound in Stonegate — which includes a dueling ground and martial training facilities — informally functions as early preparation for Legion service.

## Culture and Values

The Legion's stated values are discipline, duty, and protection — articulated in training, repeated in ceremony, embedded in rank titles. What service actually means to the people who serve is more complicated.

For most soldiers, especially in the Ironshields, service is a job with genuine weight attached to it. The uniform carries social authority. The training is demanding in ways that matter. Soldiers who make it through the Academy and find their footing often describe a real sense of belonging — the Legion provides structure, community, and purpose in a city that doesn't always offer those things outside institutions.

For the officer class — Spearlords, Garrison Commanders, members of the Council of Spears — the calculus is different. These are people navigating the intersection of military authority, noble house politics, and the ruling council's competing interests. The Legion at this level is as much a political institution as a military one. An appointment to the Council of Spears signals faction alignment; a Magister of Defense who displeases the Guild risks losing the confirmation vote; a Garrison Commander in Stonegate who antagonizes Der'Tagan doesn't hold the post long.

The harder thing to speak about is this: the institution was forged in a war that began with a genocide. Many of the soldiers who built the Legion's traditions participated in that genocide, or were commanded by the man who ordered it. The Legion carries that history in its ceremonies without carrying its meaning. The [[Drorn'Duur]] are not mentioned in Academy training. The Landing War is taught as a conflict between settlers and spirits — the Drorn'Duur's role as co-belligerents, the pact violations that gave the spirit response its legal standing, the fact that the spirits were responding to genuine grievance — none of it appears in the curriculum.

None of this requires bad people. That's the point. The ceremony does it. The oath does it. Four hundred years of both, and the soldiers who carry that legacy do so with pride and incomplete information.

## The Noble House Compact

All six original noble houses descend from companions of [[Marduk Sunspear]] — people present during the Landing War who survived it. Every founding noble house therefore has its own institutional memory of the conflict that created the Legion. How they relate to the Legion reflects what their ancestors were.

**[[House Der'Tagan]]** is the most directly military. Formed from the high-orc warriors in Marduk's company, their blood runs through Legion ranks at every level — Ironshields, Vanguard of Valor, and the Skywarden rotation in Luminous Reach. The Der'Tagan compound doubles informally as a Legion facility. The relationship is deep enough that the line between house and Legion sometimes blurs.

**[[House Chromewell]]** maintains its Legion relationship primarily through the Runeguard, whose work in spiritual risk overlaps with Chromewell expertise in spirit negotiation. Chromewell members occasionally serve as civilian advisors on Fend operations. They are the noble house most likely to push back, quietly, when the Runeguard encounters something it can't explain.

**[[House Atripho]]** has historically supplied the Nightcloaks with both intelligence contacts and personnel — though this is not formally acknowledged. The Atriphan tradition of operating from behind curtains maps comfortably onto what the Nightcloaks do.

**[[House Athram]]** provides the institutional record. Their scribes maintain the Legion's official archive and serve as officiants at major Legion ceremonies. They know what's in the Academy's institutional history curriculum and are precisely the house least likely to question it.

**[[House Cryxis]]** — raised to nobility through sacrifice at the Shattered Strand rather than descent from Marduk's companions — has a different relationship with the Legion than the founding houses. Their engagement is primarily through the Vanguard of Valor, whose mission of decisive force aligns with their pragmatic approach to civic defense.

## Traditions and Ceremonies

**The Day of Unity Procession** — Once per year, on the anniversary of the First Pact, the Magister of Defense carries Marduk's spear through Stonegate in formal procession, flanked by representatives from each division. This is the Legion's highest ceremonial moment. It is also the ceremony that most completely encapsulates the Legion's relationship to its own history: Marduk honored without examination, the Pact celebrated without its suppressed terms, the soldiers carrying that legacy with pride and incomplete information.

**The Oath of the Spear** — The ceremony at which an Initiate completes the Spear Academy and receives the rank of Sunspear. The oath invokes Marduk's memory, the First Pact, and the Legion's mission to protect Aru'Mas. The text has been revised at least three times across the city's history; the current version dates to approximately two centuries ago.

**Dawn of Solara Displays** — During the Dawn of Solara festival, the Skywardens perform aerial demonstrations above the city — military demonstration, civic celebration, and recruitment advertisement in that order of priority, though the official framing inverts it.

**Garrison Remembrance** — Within each district garrison, the death of a serving member is marked by a brief ceremony in which their sunspear weapon is displayed for one day before being melted down and incorporated into a new weapon. The new weapon is issued to the next Initiate who completes training from that garrison. The tradition predates complete documentation; its origin is attributed variously to the Landing War years and to an early Magister of Defense whose name is not preserved.

## Current State (439 MC)

The Sunspear Legion in the present day is a mature institution operating within a city that has not faced an existential military threat in generations. Its day-to-day work is patrol, enforcement, and the management of a multiversally trafficked hub. The major threats it handles are criminal networks, rogue magical entities, and periodic crises in the Fend — not the thirteen-year wars that shaped it.

This stability is both its strength and its problem. The Legion's structures were built for a city under existential pressure. Sustained through four centuries of tradition without equivalent pressure, those structures have calcified in places. Promotion within the Council of Spears sometimes reflects political connection as much as operational merit. Garrison Commanders in quiet districts develop comfortable relationships with the interests they nominally oversee. The elite divisions remain genuinely capable, but the Ironshields in some areas have become as much a neighborhood presence as a military one — which is mostly fine, and occasionally is not.

Prince [[Warrior Prince|Jorran Ilderas]] (18) currently trains with the city guard in Stonegate, an arrangement that pleases [[House Der'Tagan]] considerably and makes the political dynamics between the royal family and the noble house visible in miniature. Whether Jorran intends to eventually lead the Legion's defense apparatus is a matter of active speculation in the Council.

The current Magister of Defense is **Feldmarch Derrivus Calcinius**. He was born in [[Ragmarket]]. He ran with a street gang called the **Iron Boots** in his early years. He came to the Legion's attention through an incident that has since become something between a legend and a recruitment story: when a faction known as **[[All Are One]]** mentally influenced and drugged a platoon of Iron Boots members into attacking the city's southern gate, Calcinius held that gate alone against his former brothers until Legion reinforcements arrived. He did not look away from what he was doing. He did not leave the post.

His appointment by [[Voren Ilderas|King Voren Ilderas]] — confirmed by the Merchants' Guild and the Church, bypassing the more obvious candidates from established military families — was a deliberate signal, though what exactly it was meant to signal depends on who you ask. [[House Der'Tagan]], whose blood runs deepest in the Legion's ranks, received it with the studied graciousness of people who know how to lose gracefully. Whether that graciousness holds as Calcinius settles into the role is a matter of ongoing calibration between the house and the man.

Calcinius is someone formed by choices about loyalty — which means he thinks about loyalty more precisely than most. He came up from the streets the Ironshields patrol. He knows what it looks like when a Spillway resident sees a Legion uniform, because he was on that side of it once. He does not pretend the Legion has earned the same standing in every district it polices.

What is less certain is what he intends to do with that knowledge. He is not a man who avoids hard questions — his entire career is a demonstration of what happens when he faces one. But he also chose the institution over his own people at the moment of crisis, and has spent the years since deciding that choice was right. The Legion under Calcinius is competent, and it is led by someone who understands the gap between what it claims to be and what it is. Whether understanding that gap narrows it is the question his tenure will eventually have to answer.
